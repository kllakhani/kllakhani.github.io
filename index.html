For our project, we decided to study the relationship between air quality and poverty levels in counties across the United States. Our main question is whether poorer counties tend to experience worse air quality. We want to know if there is a meaningful correlation between a countys poverty rate and the pollution that people living there are exposed to.

This topic fits well with both of our academic interests. From a data and technology perspective, it gives us a chance to work with real datasets that require cleaning, merging, and careful analysis. On the environmental side, it connects directly to issues of public health and environmental justice. Air quality is not only an environmental concern but also a social one because poor air can cause asthma, heart disease, and other long-term health problems. If those health risks fall more heavily on low-income communities, it raises important questions about fairness and how resources are distributed in the United States.

We are also motivated by what has already been documented in environmental justice research. Many studies have found that low-income neighborhoods and historically marginalized communities often live closer to highways, factories, and other pollution sources. By looking at national data from 2023, we want to see whether these patterns still show up and how strong the relationship is when we compare poverty levels and air quality directly.

For our analysis, we chose 2023 because it is the most recent year where the datasets we are using overlap. Our plan is to clean each dataset, fix any missing or inconsistent entries, and then merge everything by county. Once we have a unified dataset, we will remove any overlapping variables that might confuse the results. After that, we will visualize the data and run some statistical tests to see if any patterns stand out.

Our goal is to find out whether counties with higher poverty rates tend to have worse air quality. If we do find a clear trend, it would support the idea that environmental burdens are not evenly shared. It would also show that pollution affects certain communities more than others, often in ways that go unnoticed. By focusing on this connection, we hope to highlight how environmental and economic issues are linked and why it is important to consider both when thinking about public health and policy.

Hypothesis

Based on past environmental justice research, our initial hypothesis was that higher-poverty counties will have slightly worse air quality, especially for pollutants like NO that are linked to traffic and industry.

We didnt expect a perfect linear trend, but we hypothesized that there might be at least a small positive relationship.

Research Questions

  1. Is there a correlation between a countys poverty rate and its air quality?

  2. Do certain regions show stronger relationships between poverty and pollution?

  3. Are counties with higher poverty exposed to worse pollutants like ozone or NO?

def norm_name(s):
    if pd.isna(s): return s
    s = str(s).strip()
    s = re.sub(r'\s+(county|parish|borough|census area|city and borough)$', '', s, flags=re.I)
    s = s.replace('St. ', 'Saint ')
    return s

We fitted a simple linear regression model to formalize the relationship between poverty and nitrogen dioxide:

$$ \text{NO}_2\_{\text{county}} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{poverty\_rate} + \varepsilon $$

Poverty_rate is the percentage of residents in poverty, and no2_am_ppb is the annual mean NO concentration in parts per billion.

The fitted model shows:

  • A slope () very close to 0, meaning that each additional percentage point of poverty changes NO levels only slightly.
  • A correlation coefficient r with small magnitude.
  • A p-value that is not strong enough to claim a clear linear relationship at conventional significance levels.

Basically, this model does not find strong evidence that county-level NO concentrations systematically increase as poverty rises. That does not prove there is no environmental injustice; it just means this particular combination of data, year, and spatial scale does not show a big linear effect.

Next, we turned our ozone question into a two-group hypothesis test. We split counties into:

  • Low-poverty: counties at or below the median poverty rate.
  • High-poverty: counties above the median.

We then compared the mean ozone level (o3_8_hr_ppm) for the two groups using a Welchs t-test. The output shows:

  • The mean ozone levels in the low- and high-poverty groups are extremely similar.
  • The p-value is not small enough to reject the null hypothesis that the two means are equal.

Under this model, we did not find a statistically significant difference in average ozone between higher-poverty and lower-poverty counties for 2023. Again, it may be that ozone is governed more by regional weather patterns and regulatory standards than by poverty alone, at least at the county scale.

Overall Findings

Going in, we expected that counties with higher poverty would have slightly worse air quality, especially for traffic-related pollutants like NO. The actual results ended up being more muted than we thought.

For ozone (O), both the scatterplot and the correlation were basically flat. The Pearson correlation between poverty rate and ozone was very close to zero, and when we ran a two-sample t-test comparing high-poverty and low-poverty counties, the mean ozone levels were almost identical. The p-value from the t-test was large, so we do not have evidence that higher-poverty counties have systematically higher ozone in this 2023 county-level sample.

For NO, we went a bit deeper and fit a simple linear regression with NO concentration as the outcome and poverty rate as the predictor. The slope was small and slightly negative, the R-squared was only about 0.04, and the p-value on the poverty coefficient was not significant. In other words, the model does not show a strong linear relationship between county poverty and NO either.

We did, however, see a more noticeable relationship between ozone and NO themselves. The correlation between those two pollutants was moderate and positive, which fits the idea that they often come from similar sources like traffic and urban activity. So the pollutants move together more clearly than either one moves with poverty in this dataset.

Limitations and Interpretation

Taken at face value, our results suggest that at the county level, poverty alone is not a strong predictor of ozone or NO in 2023. That does not mean environmental injustice is not real; it says more about the scale and structure of the data we used.

There are a few important limitations:

  • Small sample for NO. After cleaning and dropping missing values, we only had 19 counties with usable NO data. With such a small sample, it is hard to detect anything but very large effects.
  • County is a coarse spatial unit. Environmental justice patterns often show up at the neighborhood or census-tract level. County averages can smooth over sharp differences within cities, so inequalities inside a county will be invisible in this analysis.
  • Limited variation in ozone. Ozone concentrations were tightly clustered in a narrow range. When a variable barely moves, even a real relationship is hard to pick up statistically.
  • One year of data. We only looked at 2023. Air quality and economic conditions can change over time, and a multi-year analysis might tell a different story.

Because of these limitations, our findings are better read as: with this particular dataset and at this scale, we do not see a strong linear relationship between county-level poverty and these specific air pollutants. That is different from saying there is no environmental injustice, and it actually motivates collecting more detailed data.

All in all, our analysis shows that the relationship between poverty and air quality is more complex than expected, and that future work would benefit from more granular geographic data.
