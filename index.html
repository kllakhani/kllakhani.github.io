For our project, we decided to observe the correlation between air quality and poverty levels in counties across the United States. We want to determine if there is a significant relationship between the level of poverty of a county and the air quality in that county.

The topic aligns with both of our majors/interests, so it seemed like a natural fit. From the technological standpoint, our primary focus will be with managing and analyzing the data; from the environmental perspective we will consider the impact of the findings regarding relationship between poverty and public health (specifically around pollution).

For our analysis, we are using 2023 data since that is the last year that these datasets overlap. Our goal is to clean, merge, and then visualize the data stripped of intersecting variables to see if a trend becomes apparent. If we are able to uncover that lower income areas face worst air pollution outcomes, it will demonstrate a relationship between poverty and environment that often goes unnoticed.

Research Questions

  1. Is there a correlation between a countys poverty rate and its air quality?

  2. Do certain regions show stronger relationships between poverty and pollution?

  3. Are counties with higher poverty exposed to worse pollutants like ozone or NO?

Initial Observations

From a preliminary look at the data, we expect to find that counties with higher poverty levels tend to have slightly worse air quality. This may not hold true for every region but earlier studies and environmental data suggest that poorer and more industrialized counties often face higher levels of pollution.

We want to confirm this by calculating the correlation between poverty rate and AQI. If the result shows a positive correlation, it would mean that as poverty increases, air quality worsens.

Extraction, Transform, and Load (ETL)

We imported two datasets from the USDA and EPA. The main challenges occurred during the cleaning and alignment process:

County names were inconsistent as some counties included suffixes such as County and Parish". We removed these to avoid merge mismatches and normalized names (ex. St. became Saint).

Pollution values included non-numeric entries. The EPA dataset uses ND to mark missing values. These were replaced with NaN and properly converted to numeric types.

FIPS codes in the EPA data were stored as floats, but we padded them to 5-digit strings so counties could be matched correctly.

Column names were messy, so we standardized them to lowercase snake_case and removed special characters.

After cleaning, both datasets merged cleanly on state and county and produced a tidy dataset that includes poverty rate, ozone levels, and NO levels.

def norm_name(s):
    if pd.isna(s): return s
    s = str(s).strip()
    s = re.sub(r'\s+(county|parish|borough|census area|city and borough)$', '', s, flags=re.I)
    s = s.replace('St. ', 'Saint ')
    return s

poverty = poverty.rename(columns={'povertyrate':'poverty_rate'})
